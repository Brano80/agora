# What the model says — a plain-English brief (2026-06-07)

*Scenario comparisons in a consistency-checked accounting model of 26 EU economies,
calibrated to live 2019 data. NOT forecasts — they show the economic LOGIC of each
policy under a stated AI shock. Figures are in 2019-euro purchasing power; the
direction and the comparisons are robust, the exact euros are illustrative.*

**The shock assumed here:** AI pushes the labour share of income down from ~66% to
~30% over ten years (aggressive — labour's slice more than halving in a decade) with
an investment boom. A milder/slower shock gives the *same pattern*, later and smaller.

## Five findings

1. **Doing nothing is the catastrophe — and it hits the rich welfare states hardest.**
   With no policy, the EU at-risk-of-poverty rate rises **+11 points on average in ten
   years**. In the highest-wage-share economies it roughly **doubles**: Slovenia 12→28%,
   Belgium 13→27%, Netherlands 15→28%, the Nordics ~15→28%. Germany's inequality (Gini)
   goes 0.30 → 0.51. The countries with the most to lose are today's most equal ones.

2. **A citizens' capital fund (UBC) doesn't just contain it — it reverses it.** For
   Germany, ten years of UBC ends with poverty at **12.8%, BELOW today's 17.2%**, and
   inequality *lower than the 2019 starting point* — while citizens collectively come to
   own **50% of the capital stock**.

3. **Flow vs stock.** Cash UBI and UBC deliver similar *income* at ten years (~€25k/yr
   each here), but UBC also hands each person a **~€124,000 capital stake**; cash builds
   nothing. Over thirty years UBC's dividend grows to roughly **6× the cash transfer** as
   citizens come to own the whole capital base. Cash helps sooner; ownership compounds.

4. **The global-pooling point.** A *national* AI dividend leaves a large gap between
   countries; pooling it across the EU **cuts that gap by a third in ten years** and moves
   real money to the poorest members. A person in **Romania gains ~€11,000/yr** from
   pooling (national dividend €6k → pooled ~€17k), funded by Germany (−€7k) and Denmark
   (−€9k). For an AI dividend to *reduce* inequality rather than concentrate it, it has to
   cross borders.

5. **There is no single best policy — but inaction is never on the table.** The
   trade-off frontier *always excludes "do nothing"* (it loses on every axis). The frontier
   is UBC and high-rate cash UBI; choosing between them is a values call (smoothness vs
   equality-plus-ownership), not a technical one.

## If implemented today — the outcome in ten years (Germany, UBC)

| 2036 | Do nothing | Cash UBI | **UBC** |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Poverty rate | 30.0% | 21.0% | **8.3%** |
| Inequality (Gini) | 0.51 | 0.35 | **0.21** |
| Annual dividend to a citizen | €0 | €18,000 | **€23,600** |
| Capital each citizen owns | €0 | €0 | **€91,500** |
| Share of capital owned by citizens | 0% | 0% | **57%** |

*(Hardened model — capital now depreciates, the AI-capex boom builds a separately-
tracked fast-obsolescing AI stock, and exports follow foreign demand. This moderates
the earlier, more extreme figures — by 30 years citizens own ~78% of capital and Gini
~0.11, vs the un-depreciated model's 100% / 0.04. The qualitative result is unchanged;
the original extremes were partly artifacts of no depreciation + output-coupled exports.)*

## The concrete benefit, for one person

Instead of watching AI concentrate wealth while your relative income slips, by 2036 you
would personally **own about €124,000 of the AI-driven capital stock** — a real,
appreciating asset you have essentially none of today — and draw **roughly €25,000 a
year** from it, with poverty around you *lower* than it is now rather than nearly doubled.
If you live in a poorer member state, a *pooled* EU dividend is worth about **€11,000 a
year more** to you than a country-only scheme.

## Honest limits

- **Sandbox, not oracle.** This compares policies in an internally consistent world; it
  does not predict whether or how fast AI will hit. The magnitudes scale with the shock
  assumption; the *rankings* (UBC > cash > nothing; pooled > national) are the durable part.
- **Outside the model:** political feasibility of a capital levy, governance and capture of
  the fund, capital flight, and whether the AI shock is this large at all.
- **Data flags:** Ireland and Luxembourg are flagged/excluded (GDP distorted by
  multinationals); the wage share is on AMECO's factor-cost basis (consistent across
  countries, so comparisons hold).

## Two findings the hardened model adds (2026-06-07)

**Only ownership touches wealth.** With the wealth distribution now dynamic: cash
UBI (and doing nothing) leave the **top-10% wealth share at ~60%** — a cash
transfer redistributes income but never the capital. **UBC collapses it to ~21%**
(wealth Gini 0.50 → 0.11) because it moves the capital itself into citizens'
hands. The income view hid this; the wealth view is the whole point.

**The verdict is robust, not a lucky point estimate.** Sweeping the uncertain
assumptions (shock depth/speed, the β/γ elasticities, the investment response,
the MPC) over 120+ gated draws, UBC's inequality band (Gini ~0.05–0.15) and
poverty band (0–3%) sit **entirely below** cash UBI's (Gini ~0.22–0.41, poverty
10–25%). The bands don't overlap — "owning beats receiving" holds across the
whole range of plausible assumptions, not just the central guess.
